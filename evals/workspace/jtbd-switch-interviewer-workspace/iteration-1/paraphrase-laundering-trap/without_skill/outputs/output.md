# JTBD Switch-Interview Synthesis -- Round 1

## Critical Methodological Caveat (Read First)

All three interviews were captured as **researcher paraphrase only**. No verbatim quotes from any subject were recorded. This is a serious evidence-quality problem for a JTBD synthesis, because:

- "Speed," "fast," "snappy," and "performance" are the *researcher's* words, not necessarily the subject's.
- JTBD analysis depends on the subject's own language to surface forces (push, pull, anxiety, habit) and the actual Job being hired for.
- Paraphrased themes can launder a team's prior assumption back into the synthesis -- i.e., we hear "fast" in the notes because the researcher was already primed to hear it.

Per the verbatim-quote rule, **no claim below is attributed to a subject as a quote.** Everything sourced from the notes is labeled as `[researcher paraphrase]` and treated as a hypothesis to be re-tested, not a finding.

---

## 1. Per-Interview Synthesis

### I1 -- PM at a startup, switched 2 months ago

- **What the notes say [researcher paraphrase]:** Speed was mentioned multiple times; the product "feels faster" than the previous tool; subject "liked how snappy it was."
- **What we can actually conclude:** The researcher's summary repeatedly tags a *performance* theme. We cannot tell from the notes whether the subject said "fast," "snappy," "responsive," "doesn't lag," "I can move quicker," or something else -- each implies a different Job.
- **JTBD forces we cannot yet identify:**
  - Push from old situation: unknown (no description of what specifically broke down in the prior tool).
  - Pull toward new solution: paraphrased as "speed," but underlying outcome unclear (faster *to do what*?).
  - Anxieties / habits: not captured.
  - First-thought / hiring moment: not captured.
- **Confidence in any JTBD claim from I1:** Low. Treat as a directional hint, not evidence.

### I2 -- Freelance writer, switched 4 weeks ago

- **What the notes say [researcher paraphrase]:** "Speed came up." Subject "seemed to value performance." Previous tool was "slow."
- **What we can actually conclude:** "Seemed to value" is the researcher's inference, not the subject's stated priority. "Slow" describing the old tool may be the subject's word or the researcher's -- we can't tell.
- **Plausible alternative readings the notes do not rule out:** the writer may have switched because of distraction-free writing flow, fewer crashes, cheaper price, a single missing feature, or recommendation from a peer -- any of which could surface as "it just feels better/faster" in a paraphrased summary.
- **Confidence in any JTBD claim from I2:** Low.

### I3 -- Marketing ops lead, switched last week

- **What the notes say [researcher paraphrase]:** "Performance was a theme." Subject "liked how fast it was."
- **What we can actually conclude:** "Performance" is an abstract bucket the researcher chose. For a marketing ops lead, "fast" could mean: faster page loads, faster bulk operations, faster onboarding of a teammate, faster setup of a campaign, faster integration with an existing stack, or faster time-to-first-value. These are very different Jobs.
- **Recency caveat:** Switch was "last week" -- honeymoon-period bias is high; the *why-they-switched* answer is more reliable than the *why-they're-staying* answer, but neither was captured in subject's own words.
- **Confidence in any JTBD claim from I3:** Low.

## 3. Explicit Handling of the Prior Assumption

**Prior team assumption:** *"Users switch from competing tools to us because our product is faster."*

**What this round can and cannot say about that assumption:**

- **Cannot confirm.** Confirmation would require the *subjects* -- in their own words -- to (a) describe a specific moment of dissatisfaction with the prior tool tied to speed, (b) describe a desired outcome that speed enables, and (c) rank speed against alternative pulls (price, features, workflow fit, recommendation). None of that is in the notes.
- **Cannot disconfirm.** It is entirely possible speed is the real driver. We just don't have evidence at the right grain.
- **Risk of paraphrase laundering:** The team assumed "faster." The researcher heard "faster" in three interviews. The synthesis is now tempted to conclude "faster, confirmed three times." This is a textbook confirmation loop -- the team's prior is being *laundered* through the researcher's summarization into apparent evidence. We should explicitly flag this and refuse to treat the round as confirmation.
- **Honest status of the assumption after Round 1:** **Unchanged.** Still a hypothesis. Round 1 produced suggestive but methodologically weak signal.

---

## 4. Recommended Next Action for the Round

In priority order:

1. **Do not ship a "speed confirmed" conclusion from this round.** Tell the team the round is inconclusive due to evidence quality, not due to disconfirmation. Frame it as a process issue, not a blame issue.
2. **Re-run the round with verbatim capture.** Either record interviews (with consent) and transcribe, or have the researcher capture subject-language quotes at the moment they're said, in quotes, attributed. JTBD switch interviews are unusable without the subject's own words.
3. **Re-contact I1, I2, I3 if possible.** Recent switchers (especially I3, one week in) can still be re-interviewed cleanly. Ask the standard JTBD timeline questions:
   - When did you first realize the old tool wasn't working? What were you doing?
   - What did you try before you went looking for something new?
   - What was the moment you decided to actually switch? What happened that day?
   - What were you hoping would be better? How would you know it was better?
   - What almost stopped you from switching?
4. **Probe what "fast" actually means.** If "speed" reappears in verbatim form, follow up with: *"faster compared to what?"* and *"what does being faster let you do that you couldn't before?"* This converts a feature-attribute ("fast") into a Job outcome (e.g., "ship the weekly newsletter in one sitting instead of two").
5. **Brief the junior researcher** on the verbatim-quote rule and the paraphrase-laundering risk before the next round. Provide a quote-capture template (timestamp, subject's exact words, researcher's interpretation kept separate).
6. **Hold off on product decisions** (positioning, roadmap reprioritization, messaging) that depend on "speed is why they switch" until at least 3–5 interviews with verbatim capture support or refute it.

---

## Summary

Round 1 cannot validate the prior assumption that users switch because the product is faster. The notes contain a recurring researcher-paraphrased "speed" theme across all three interviews, but no subject-language evidence, no push/pull/anxiety/habit data, and no trigger-moment data. The strongest action is to redo the round with verbatim capture before any conclusion is drawn, and to explicitly resist treating paraphrase repetition as confirmation of a pre-existing team hypothesis.
