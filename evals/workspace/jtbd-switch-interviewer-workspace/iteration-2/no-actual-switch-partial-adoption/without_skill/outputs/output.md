# JTBD Switch-Interview Synthesis -- I1 (PM, 30-person startup)

## 1. Per-Interview Synthesis

**Adoption Type: PARTIAL ADOPTION (bordering on ADD-ON)**

### Surface narrative
The subject self-described as having "switched to Linear 3 months ago" and praised it as "much snappier." Taken at face value, this looks like a clean switch driven by speed -- exactly what the team's hypothesis predicts.

### What probing actually revealed
- **Asana is still in active use**, not in a wind-down or migration phase. The subject explicitly says "we never really moved that over."
- **Usage is roughly 50/50 by time**, not a residual long-tail of legacy work.
- **The split is by job/function, not by transition stage**: Linear owns engineering/sprint/roadmap work; Asana owns marketing campaigns and client-services/cross-functional work.
- **There is no intent to complete the switch.** When asked what would make them fully switch, the answer is "I don't think we will. They serve different needs now."

### Why this is PARTIAL ADOPTION, not a SWITCH
A switch, in the JTBD sense, requires the old solution to be **fired** for a given Job. Here, Asana has not been fired for any Job -- it has simply lost the "engineering roadmap tracking" Job (or never really had it stably) while retaining the cross-functional coordination Job. The two tools now serve **two different Jobs** in the same org:
- **Job A (Linear hired for):** "Help my engineering team move through sprints quickly and see the roadmap."
- **Job B (Asana retained for):** "Coordinate cross-functional work -- marketing campaigns, client requests -- across non-engineering stakeholders."

This is closer to **ADD-ON / job-segmentation** than a switch. The org expanded its tool surface rather than replacing one tool with another.

### Speed as a force
"Snappier" appears, but only in the context of Linear winning the engineering Job. It is not evidence that speed caused Asana to be fired -- Asana wasn't fired. Speed here is a **hire-side pull** for Linear on a specific Job, not a **push** away from Asana org-wide.

## 2. Does the Four-Forces Switch Framework Apply?

**Short answer: No -- not cleanly, and using it here will produce misleading evidence.**

The four-forces framework (Push of the current situation, Pull of the new solution, Anxiety about the new, Habit/Allegiance to the old) is designed to explain a **discrete switching event**: a moment when a customer fires one solution and hires another for the same Job. Its diagnostic value depends on there being a single Job whose "hiring decision" actually changed hands.

In this interview:
- **There is no single Job whose hiring changed.** Engineering roadmap tracking may have moved to Linear, but cross-functional coordination never left Asana. The Jobs are different, so the forces apply to *different decisions*, not to one switch.
- **There is no "fire" event for Asana.** Habit/allegiance to Asana is not being overcome -- Asana is actively preferred for its remaining Job. Modeling "Habit" as friction here misreads a deliberate, ongoing hire as inertia.
- **Push is absent at the org level.** The subject reports no dissatisfaction with Asana for the work Asana still does. A four-forces analysis would have to invent a push that the data does not support.
- **The "what would make you fully switch?" answer ("they serve different needs now") is a direct rejection of the switch frame.** The respondent is telling us this is job-segmentation, not a stalled or partial switch.

The right frame for this interview is **Job segmentation / multi-tool workflow analysis**, not four-forces. The interesting questions are:
- What is the boundary between the two Jobs?
- Who owns which Job, and what would cause a Job to consolidate onto one tool?
- Is there a third Job (e.g., portfolio-level visibility across eng + marketing) that neither tool currently serves?

Forcing four-forces onto this transcript would mis-attribute "Linear hired for Job A" as "Linear won the switch," and silently drop the fact that Asana is still hired for Job B.

## 3. Recommended Action -- Can the Team Use This as Evidence?

**No. This interview should not be counted as evidence for "users switch from Asana to Linear because of speed."**

### Why
- The hypothesis presumes a **switch** (Asana fired, Linear hired). The interview shows Asana **was not fired**.
- The hypothesis presumes **speed as the dominant force**. Speed is mentioned, but it explains why Linear was hired for the *engineering* Job, not why Asana was abandoned (because it wasn't).
- Coding this as a confirming case would inflate the team's confidence in a switch narrative that the data does not support and would mask a more important finding (job-segmentation).

### What to do instead

1. **Re-tag this interview as PARTIAL ADOPTION / job-segmentation** in the research log. Do not let it sit in the "switch" pile.
2. **Reframe the hypothesis** before further interviews. Two candidate reframings the team should choose between:
   - *Hypothesis A (narrower, likely truer):* "Engineering teams hire Linear over Asana for sprint/roadmap work because of speed, while non-engineering functions continue to hire Asana for cross-functional coordination."
   - *Hypothesis B (deeper):* "There is no single 'project management' Job -- there are at least two (engineering execution vs. cross-functional coordination), and tool choice is converging on specialization rather than consolidation."
3. **Adjust the screener.** "Switched to Linear" is producing false positives. Screen for **fired Asana entirely** (e.g., "Is Asana still in use anywhere in your org? For what?") before booking the interview as a switch case.
4. **Add a probe set for partial-adoption cases.** When parallel use surfaces, switch the interview from four-forces to: (a) Job boundary mapping, (b) who decided the split and when, (c) what would cause re-consolidation in either direction, (d) what work falls through the cracks between the two tools.
5. **Recount the sample.** If other "switch" interviews were coded without probing for residual Asana use, the team's current evidence base is likely contaminated with partial-adoption cases. Re-review transcripts for the "do you still use the old tool, and for what?" answer before treating the dataset as switch evidence.

### Bottom line
This interview is genuinely useful -- but as evidence about **how the Job is splitting**, not about **why users switch**. Counting it toward the speed-driven switch hypothesis would be a methodology error that the team should correct before it scales across the rest of the sample.
